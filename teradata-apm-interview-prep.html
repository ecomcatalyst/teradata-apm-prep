"Walk me through how you would write a user story for a new feature on our analytics platform."
⚡ This tests documentation skill — a core APM responsibility per the JD.
Framework: Template → Example → Success Criteria
"I follow the standard user story format: 'As a [persona], I want to [action] so that [outcome].' But I always add three things that make it actually useful for engineering: acceptance criteria, edge cases, and success metrics.

Let me give you a live example relevant to Teradata. Say we're building a natural language query feature:

User Story: As a business analyst who doesn't write SQL, I want to type a question in plain English and see a data result, so that I can get answers from my data without waiting for a data engineer.

Acceptance Criteria:
• The system interprets the query and generates a SQL statement
• The SQL is displayed alongside the result so the analyst can verify logic
• If the query is ambiguous, 2–3 clarifying options are surfaced before execution
• Query executes within 5 seconds for datasets under 100GB

Edge Cases: Vague queries like 'How are we doing?' should surface disambiguation options, not guess. Queries on restricted tables should return a permission error, not empty results.

Success Metric: % of queries completed without manual SQL correction within 30 days of launch.
The key discipline: the story isn't done until you can answer 'how will we know this is working?' — that's the success metric, and it should be defined before a single line of code is written."

Q3Situational
"A senior PM asks you to track product usage for a new analytics feature and identify improvement opportunities. How do you approach this?"
⚡ Directly from the JD: "Track product usage and gather customer feedback to identify improvement opportunities."
Framework: Clarify → Instrument → Analyze → Synthesize → Recommend
"Before I start pulling data, I'd clarify three things: What does 'usage' mean for this feature? (sessions? actions taken? outcomes achieved?), What's the time window?, and What decisions will this data inform?

Then I'd work through 5 steps:

Step 1 – Define metrics: Adoption rate (% of eligible users who tried the feature), activation rate (% who completed a meaningful action), retention (did they come back?), error rate (how often did it fail?), and time-to-value (how long from first use to first meaningful output?).

Step 2 – Instrument: Confirm event tracking is set up correctly in the analytics tool. At Enigma I caught several tracking gaps that were making our reports misleading — verifying instrumentation is step zero.

Step 3 – Cohort analysis: Segment users by persona, onboarding path, and tenure. Patterns that appear in aggregates often disappear or reverse at segment level.

Step 4 – Qualitative triangulation: Pull 5–10 support tickets related to the feature. Run a quick survey or 3 user interviews. Data tells you what is happening; users tell you why.

Step 5 – Synthesize into a 1-page brief: What are the top 3 improvement opportunities, what data supports each, and what's my recommended next action?

I'd share a draft with the senior PM before finalizing — their domain context will sharpen the 'so what' significantly."

Q4Product Thinking
"How would you prioritize 5 feature requests from different customers for a quarterly roadmap?"
Framework: RICE + Strategic filter
"I'd use a two-stage approach: quantitative scoring + strategic filter.

Stage 1 – RICE scoring:
• Reach: How many customers/users does this affect? (not just the one who asked)
• Impact: How significantly does it improve their core job? (1–3 scale)
• Confidence: How validated is this need? (just one customer request vs. pattern across 10?)
• Effort: Engineering estimate in sprints
RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort → gives a comparable score

Stage 2 – Strategic filter: Before finalizing, I check each high-scoring item against: Does this advance our platform's AI/analytics direction? Does it help with customer retention risk? Is there a commitment to a key account?

What I'd NOT do: prioritize by whoever asked loudest, or by which customer is biggest if the feature only helps them. One customer's urgent request shouldn't derail platform-level work.

Then I'd put the ranked list in front of the senior PM with my reasoning visible — they'll override some decisions, and that's fine. My job is to make the best-reasoned recommendation, not to make the final call."

Q3Judgment
"You're supporting a senior PM who disagrees with your analysis. You believe your data is right. What do you do?"
Framework: Disagree and commit — with the right sequence
"I'd follow a 'disagree → understand → decide → commit' sequence.

First, I make sure I actually understand their disagreement. 'Can you help me understand what in the data you're seeing differently, or is it more about the conclusion I'm drawing?' Sometimes what looks like a data disagreement is actually a disagreement about assumptions — and those are important to surface.

Second, I present my reasoning one more time — clearly and concisely, with the specific data point that leads me to my conclusion. Not arguing. Presenting.

Third, if we still disagree after that conversation, I defer and commit. They have more context — about the customer, about the strategic context, about what I might not know yet. I'd say: 'I want to note my concern for the record, and I'd love to track this with data after we ship — but I'm fully aligned with moving forward with your direction.'

Fourth, I set up the measurement. If I think the data points one way and we went the other, I make sure we have the instrumentation to learn who was right — not to win an argument, but because that's how both of us get better.

Being a great APM means being the PM's force multiplier, not their critic. You earn the right to be heard more by being right on the small things, consistently, over time."

Q5: "What do you think is the biggest challenge for enterprise analytics platforms right now?"
"The trust gap between AI output and enterprise decision-makers. It's not a technical problem anymore — models are capable enough. The challenge is that data analysts and executives in regulated industries (finance, healthcare) can't use AI-generated insights if they can't audit how the AI got there. The winning platform won't just have the most accurate AI — it'll have the most transparent AI. Explainability, audit trails, and confidence signals built directly into the UX. That's the product challenge I find most interesting about Teradata's current direction."
Q6: "How do you balance speed of shipping with quality?"
"Context-dependent. For a new feature in a low-stakes area — move fast, accept rough edges, ship, learn. For anything touching enterprise customer data pipelines or compliance — quality is non-negotiable and speed is secondary. I've learned to ask two questions before any launch: 'What's the worst thing that happens if this breaks?' and 'Who finds out first?' Those answers calibrate how much quality investment is justified."
Q7: "What would you do in your first 30 days at Teradata?"
"Week 1: Listen and learn. Read every PRD, research doc, and roadmap item I can access. Map the team structure and understand who owns what. Week 2: Start contributing — pick up the smallest, clearest tasks. Deliver one thing well before taking on anything complex. Week 3–4: Begin building my own context: trial the product as an analyst persona, compile a competitive snapshot on one specific feature, and have 1:1s with CS, Sales, and at least one customer if possible. By day 30, I want to have one concrete output the senior PM can use — and a clear picture of where I can add the most value in Q2."
Q8: "Tell me about a product you use daily and what you'd improve about it."
"Notion — I use it constantly. The thing I'd improve is the 'new page' experience. When you hit 'New page,' you start from a completely blank canvas with a cursor. For 90% of use cases, users have a category in mind — meeting notes, project brief, personal journal. I'd add a contextual template suggestion: a lightweight overlay that shows 3–5 suggested templates based on where in your workspace you are, that can be dismissed in one click. The 'aha moment' for Notion is when your workspace starts feeling like a second brain — but the blank page is the biggest barrier to building that habit. Reduce the setup cost of each new page and you increase the velocity of thought capture."
Q9: "How would you measure whether our product roadmap is working?"
"Three levels: (1) Output: Are we shipping what we planned, on time? (2) Outcome: Are the shipped features achieving their defined success metrics? Adoption, retention, time-to-value. (3) Impact: Are we moving the needle on company-level metrics — NRR (net revenue retention), customer health scores, expansion revenue? A roadmap that ships on time but doesn't move outcomes is a project plan, not a product strategy. The third level — impact — is the one that matters for a Teradata senior PM's bonus."
Q10: "Describe a failure in your product work and what you learned."
"At Aasaan, I built a feature specification for a merchant bulk upload tool based primarily on a request from one large merchant. I didn't validate the need across a broader user sample before committing engineering time. The feature shipped, and adoption was about 15% of what we expected — turns out the large merchant's workflow was unusual, and most merchants didn't have the data volume that made bulk upload valuable. The lesson: one loud customer voice is a hypothesis, not a requirement. I now require at least 3 independent sources — usage data, tickets, and interviews — before I'll commit engineering resources to a feature."
Q11: "How do you stay current on AI trends relevant to product management?"
"Three sources I rely on: (1) Primary — I build things. I run n8n workflows, I've set up local LLM inference with Ollama, I built a full Android app using Jetpack Compose. Hands-on beats reading. (2) Curated reading — I follow Lenny's Newsletter, Reforge content, and Anthropic/OpenAI research blogs. (3) Community — I participate in APM communities and follow PMs at AI-native companies on LinkedIn. For Teradata specifically, I've been tracking your ClearScape Analytics changelogs and the analyst commentary around your agentic AI pivot. Staying current is a discipline, not a hobby."
Q12: "What questions do you ask when you receive a feature request?"
"Five questions, always: (1) Who is experiencing this problem, and how many of them? (2) How are they solving it today without this feature? (3) What does success look like — what would they do differently if this existed? (4) How urgent is this? What happens if we ship it in 3 months vs. 3 weeks? (5) Is this a symptom of a deeper underlying problem? The last question is the most important — sometimes a 'feature request' is actually the user's best guess at a solution to a problem we should solve differently."
Q13: "What does good look like for an APM 6 months in?"
"Six months in, good looks like: (1) I'm trusted to own small scopes end-to-end — a user story, a research brief, a demo maintenance cycle — without the senior PM needing to check my work. (2) I'm adding context the senior PM doesn't have — I know the customer feedback better, I know the competitive landscape better, I know the usage data better. (3) I'm proactively flagging risks before they become problems. (4) I've built relationships across engineering and design — people message me directly because they know I'll be useful, not a blocker. That's the APM I want to be 6 months from now."
Q14: "Is there anything about your background that you're concerned might not be a fit?"
"Honest answer: I haven't worked at the scale Teradata operates — petabyte-scale data warehouses, global enterprise customers, multi-cloud infrastructure. That's a gap I know exists and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. What I'd say is: my gap is domain depth, not product fundamentals. I know how to write a PRD, conduct research, analyze data, work with engineering, and think from the user's perspective — I've done all of that. What I'll need to learn is the Teradata platform deeply, the enterprise customer dynamics, and the technical vocabulary of the data warehouse world. I'm a fast learner, I'm already ahead on that curve given my AI proficiency prep, and I'm genuinely excited about the domain. I'll earn the domain knowledge — but the PM fundamentals are already there."

Story Delivery Tips
How to Tell a Story That Lands
⏱️ Timing
90–120 seconds per story. Not 4 minutes. If you're going longer, you're overexplaining the situation and under-delivering the result. Practice with a timer.
📊 Lead with the result once
In round 3 especially: open with the result, then tell the story. "I led an initiative that reduced churn by 21.8% — here's how." Anchors the story in impact immediately.
🎯 Say "I" not "we"
Interviewers need to know what you specifically did. "We reduced churn" → "I analyzed the cohort data and identified that users who missed 3 core actions in week 1 had 4× higher churn — I then drafted the PRD and worked with design to ship the revised onboarding."
🔢 Quantify everything you can
Numbers create credibility. 21.8% > "significantly." 4× > "much higher." Even rough estimates with stated assumptions beat vague language.
