P·01
How would you prioritise features for a new analytics product? Walk me through your framework.
Prioritisation
▼
Ideal Answer

"Prioritisation for me starts with three questions: What's the user's most painful unsolved problem? What moves the north star metric? What can we ship fast enough to validate before it becomes a bigger bet?"

My Framework (Contextual, Not Dogmatic) Step 1 — Define the north star: For an analytics product, it's usually something like "insights activated per user" or "models in production per customer." Everything else is a proxy.

Step 2 — Score opportunities: I use a modified RICE (Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort) with a data quality weight — in analytics products, features that improve data trust often unlock downstream value disproportionate to their apparent impact score.

Step 3 — Sequence for learning: I front-load features that resolve the most uncertainty about user behavior, not just the highest-RICE items. Wrong assumptions compound.

Step 4 — Gut-check with stakeholders: I circulate the prioritised backlog with rationale, invite challenge, and document disagreements. Transparency makes tradeoffs defensible.

"At Teradata's scale, I'd also weigh customer segment value — enterprise customers often have very different prioritisation needs than SMB, and a single feature that unlocks a large enterprise segment may outweigh several SMB-facing improvements."

T·01
What is ModelOps and why does it matter for enterprise AI?
ClearScapeTeradata-Specific
▼
Ideal Answer — This is Core to ClearScape

"ModelOps is the operational discipline of managing AI/ML models after they leave the data scientist's notebook — through deployment, monitoring, governance, retraining, and retirement. It's the production half of the ML lifecycle that's almost always more complex than the training half."

Why It Matters — The Enterprise Problem The dirty secret of enterprise AI: most models trained never make it to production. Of those that do, most degrade silently over time without anyone noticing until there's a business impact.

ModelOps solves:
• Deployment friction: Going from notebook to production typically requires significant reengineering. ModelOps standardizes that path.
• Model drift: Real-world data distribution changes. A fraud model trained on 2023 data may miss 2024 fraud patterns. ModelOps automates drift detection and triggers retraining.
• Governance & audit: Regulated industries (banking, insurance) need to explain model decisions. ModelOps provides the audit trail.
• Scale: Teradata's ModelOps can operationalize millions of models — think demand forecasting for every SKU at a global retailer.
T·02
What's the difference between in-database analytics and traditional ML pipelines? Why does it matter?
ClearScapeArchitecture
▼
Ideal Answer — Teradata's Core Differentiator
Traditional ML Pipeline Data sits in a warehouse/lake → Extract it → Transfer to a separate ML platform (e.g., SageMaker, Databricks) → Train model there → Push results back to the database.

Problems: Data movement is expensive (storage, compute, licensing). Latency. Security/governance gaps when data leaves the system. Complexity of maintaining two platforms.
In-Database Analytics (ClearScape Approach) Data stays in VantageCloud. Analytics functions execute directly on the data where it lives. Model trains in-database. Scoring runs in-database.

Benefits: Zero data movement cost. No latency from data transfer. Full governance maintained. Performance scales with Teradata's workload management, not a separate system.

"For a retailer forecasting demand for 10 million SKUs, the cost difference between moving that data out vs. computing in place isn't marginal — it's orders of magnitude. That's why Teradata's positioning resonates with large enterprise customers."

T·05
What is model drift and how would you detect it?
ML Concepts
▼
Ideal Answer

"Model drift is when a machine learning model's performance degrades over time because the real-world data it's scoring has changed from the data it was trained on."

Types of Drift Data drift (covariate shift): The input feature distribution changes. E.g., a credit scoring model trained pre-COVID encounters very different income patterns post-COVID.

Concept drift: The relationship between inputs and target changes. E.g., a fraud model trained on 2023 fraud patterns may miss new fraud techniques that emerged in 2024.

Label drift: The distribution of outcomes changes — e.g., if actual churn rates in your business shift seasonally.
Detection Methods • Monitor model performance metrics (accuracy, AUC, precision-recall) on a held-out validation set over time.
• Track input feature statistics (mean, variance, distribution shape) and alert when they deviate beyond a threshold.
• Use population stability index (PSI) to compare score distributions between training and current scoring windows.
• ClearScape ModelOps automates this monitoring and can trigger retraining workflows when drift thresholds are crossed.
T·06
Write a SQL query to find the top 5 customers by revenue in the last 30 days who have not placed an order in the last 7 days — these might be churn risks.
SQL
▼
Ideal Answer — Show Your SQL Fluency
SQL Query SELECT
  c.customer_id,
  c.customer_name,
  SUM(o.order_value) AS revenue_last_30d,
  MAX(o.order_date) AS last_order_date
FROM customers c
JOIN orders o ON c.customer_id = o.customer_id
WHERE o.order_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '30' DAY
GROUP BY c.customer_id, c.customer_name
HAVING MAX(o.order_date) < CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '7' DAY
ORDER BY revenue_last_30d DESC
FETCH FIRST 5 ROWS ONLY;

"In Teradata SQL specifically, FETCH FIRST n ROWS ONLY is the standard syntax for LIMIT. The HAVING clause filters post-aggregation — that's how we check the last order date after grouping. This query is useful as an early-warning list for a CSM team doing proactive outreach."

What's the difference between a supervised and unsupervised ML model? Give an example of when you'd use each in an analytics context.
ML Concepts
▼
Ideal Answer
Supervised vs Unsupervised Supervised: You have labelled training data — inputs paired with known outputs. The model learns to predict the output for new inputs.
Example in analytics: Churn prediction. You have historical records of customers who churned (label = 1) vs. retained (label = 0), plus their feature profiles. The model learns the pattern.

Unsupervised: No labels. The model finds structure or patterns in the data on its own.
Example in analytics: Customer segmentation. You don't know in advance how many customer types exist. A clustering algorithm (k-means) groups customers by behavioral similarity — then you interpret and name those clusters (e.g., "high-value loyalists," "price-sensitive casuals").

"My lead-scoring model at Aasaan was supervised — I had clear conversion data as labels. A useful unsupervised complement would have been clustering the leads first to identify natural segments, then training separate classifiers per segment for higher precision."

T·08
What is the difference between precision and recall? Which matters more for a fraud detection use case?
ML Metrics
▼
Ideal Answer
Definitions Precision: Of all transactions the model flagged as fraud, what % were actually fraud? High precision = few false alarms.

Recall: Of all actual fraud cases in reality, what % did the model catch? High recall = few missed frauds.

For fraud detection — Recall usually matters more.

"The cost of a missed fraud (false negative) — real financial loss, customer damage, regulatory exposure — is typically much higher than the cost of a false alarm (false positive, which costs analyst review time). So you want high recall: catch as much fraud as possible, even if you get some false alarms."

"However, if the precision is so low that analysts are drowning in false alarms, the system becomes unusable. In practice, you tune the decision threshold to find an acceptable precision-recall tradeoff, often guided by the business's cost ratio between missed fraud and investigation cost per alert."

"At Aasaan, I optimized for precision in my lead-scoring model — the cost of a false positive (wasting a sales rep's call) was higher than the cost of a false negative (missing a slightly lower-quality lead who might still convert)."

What is AutoML and what are its limitations?
ML ConceptsClearScape Feature
▼
Ideal Answer

"AutoML automates the process of selecting, training, and tuning machine learning models — historically the most time-intensive and expertise-dependent parts of building an ML solution. Instead of a data scientist manually trying 20 algorithm configurations, AutoML runs them systematically and surfaces the best performer."

ClearScape's AutoML specifically is designed to enable non-technical business users to kick off model training without coding, while maintaining the governance and scale of VantageCloud underneath.

Limitations of AutoML (Be Honest) Not a domain knowledge substitute: AutoML will find the best model for the data you give it. If your features are poorly engineered or your training data has label leakage, AutoML will faithfully optimize a broken model.

Black box risk: Auto-selected models may be harder to explain to regulators or business stakeholders. Explainability often requires manual intervention.

Diminishing returns: For simple problems, AutoML is overkill. For very complex or novel problems, expert data scientists will still outperform it by knowing which architectures to try.

Compute cost: AutoML's search process is computationally expensive. Important to scope it appropriately.
